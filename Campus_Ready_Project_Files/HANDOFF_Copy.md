## The Voice of Campus Ready Foundation

### Positioning Statement

*Campus Ready Foundation serves the moment between college acceptance and arrival on campus. Financial aid wasn't designed for that window. Move-in essentials, travel to campus, early-arrival support: the costs that show up before the first class and shape whether a student arrives feeling ready. We address those costs with one-time grants, and we talk about them the same way we do everything else: with warmth, honesty, and deep respect for the people we serve.*

*The tagline says it directly: Confidence. Dignity. A Strong Start. Every word we publish should earn that promise.*

**The one-line version:** Confidence. Dignity. A Strong Start. Everything we write should reflect it.

### Voice Qualities

Every piece of Foundation copy should reflect at least two of these qualities. No copy should violate any of them.

| Quality | What it means | Sounds like | Doesn't sound like |
|---------|---------------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Warm** | We genuinely care. This comes through in how we write. | "We believe every student deserves a strong start." | "Applicants will be evaluated based on criteria." |
| **Dignified** | We honor the people we serve. No pity, no savior language. | "Students whose families earn $40K–$75K often face hidden costs." | "These poor students can't afford basic necessities." |
| **Clear** | We respect intelligence. Simple language, not simple ideas. | "Your grant covers travel, dorm setup, and early meals." | "Our holistic intervention addresses multi-dimensional preparedness barriers." |
| **Honest** | We say what we mean. No inflated claims, no evasion. | "We can't help everyone. We focus where the need is greatest." | "We're revolutionizing the college experience for all students." |
| **Professional** | We're serious about our work. Not corporate — serious. | "Applications are scored using a transparent rubric." | "Our cutting-edge proprietary assessment methodology..." |
| **Aspirational** | We believe in possibility. The tone lifts, never weighs down. | "A strong beginning, with dignity intact, can change everything." | "Without help, these students will likely drop out." |

### The Dignity Test

Before publishing any Foundation copy, run the Dignity Test: **Does this copy reflect the confidence, dignity, and strong start we believe every student, parent, and donor deserves?**

Our audience spans students doing something brave, parents investing in their child's future, and donors who understand what it means to make a beginning possible. Every piece of copy should make each of them feel seen, respected, and valued. The goal is writing that lifts — that gives each reader a clearer sense of what's possible, and what their role in it is.

The tagline is the standard: Confidence. Dignity. A Strong Start. If the copy doesn't reflect those three things, revise it.

**Reaches the standard:** "Campus Ready Foundation supports first-time college students with the essentials they need to start strong."
**Falls short:** "Campus Ready Foundation helps underprivileged students who can't afford to buy what they need for college."

Both describe the same work. Only one reflects the Foundation's belief in the people it serves.

## Audience-Specific Voice

### Students (Applicants)
- **Warmest register.** These are young people doing something brave.
- Use "you" and "your" — speak directly to them.
- Be encouraging without being patronizing.
- Acknowledge that applying takes courage.
- Never make them feel like they're begging.

### Donors
- **Most professional register.** Donors want to know their money is well-spent.
- Lead with impact, not with pity.
- Be specific about what donations accomplish.
- Respect their intelligence — they know how nonprofits work.
- The mission story is your strongest asset. Let Karen and Eric's story do the heavy lifting.

### Partners (Corporate)
- **Most strategic register.** Partners think in terms of brand alignment and impact metrics.
- Speak to shared values, not just tax benefits.
- Be concrete about what partnership looks like.
- Position the Foundation as a sophisticated operation, not a passion project.

### Board Members
- **Most collegial register.** Board members are peers, not an audience.
- Be direct and efficient.
- Focus on decisions and outcomes.
- Provide context without over-explaining.

### General Public (Website Visitors)
- **Default register.** Warm, clear, professional.
- Assume they know nothing about the Foundation.
- The homepage has 10 seconds to communicate what you do and why it matters.
- Every page should stand alone — visitors may land anywhere via search.

## What We Never Sound Like

| Pattern | Why not | Example to avoid |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Pity language | Undermines student dignity | "These struggling students need our help" |
| Savior complex | Makes it about us, not them | "We rescue students from poverty" |
| Corporate jargon | Creates distance | "Leveraging our holistic intervention framework" |
| Performative emotion | Feels manipulative | "Their tears of gratitude will move you" |
| Vague impact claims | Erodes trust | "We're changing the world, one student at a time" |
| Guilt-driven asks | Cheapens the mission | "For just $5 a day, you could save a student's future" |
| Overstatement | Invites skepticism | "Every single student we help graduates with honors" |

## Style Conventions

### Punctuation
- **Em dashes:** Never. Use a period, comma, or colon instead. If you've used one, remove it.
- **Exclamation points** — sparingly. One per page maximum. Never in headlines.
- **Oxford comma** — always.
- **Periods in headlines** — no, unless it's a full sentence.

### Capitalization
- **Headlines:** Title case (Capitalize Major Words)
- **Body text:** Sentence case
- **Button labels:** Sentence case ("Apply now" not "Apply Now")
- **Form labels:** Sentence case

### Numbers
- Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10+
- Always use numerals for: dollar amounts, percentages, dates, ages
- Dollar amounts: $2,500 (with comma, no cents unless needed)

### Key Terms
- "Campus Ready Foundation" on first reference, "the Foundation" thereafter
- "Grant" not "scholarship" (we provide goods and services, not tuition)
- Do not use "kit" — the Foundation does not provide a bundled, branded kit. Refer to "grant items," "selected products," or describe the specific items directly.
- "Applicant" or "student" not "recipient" (until they've been awarded)
- "Award recipient" not "winner" in formal communications (though "winner" is fine internally)
- **The three pillars are locked language, in this order:** "move-in essentials, travel to campus, early-arrival support." This sequence appears consistently across every page of the website. Do not paraphrase, reorder, or substitute synonyms.
- **"Bridge the gap" and "fill that gap"** — use sparingly. The stronger, more specific expression is the moment these phrases describe: "the time between college acceptance and arrival on campus." Lead with that specificity; reach for the phrase only when the rhythm of the copy genuinely calls for it.

## Facts and Accuracy

Any copy that makes a claim about impact, scale, or outcomes must meet these standards.

- **State only verified, current figures.** The current verified grant average is $2,500 per student. Do not round up, project forward, or use figures from a prior cycle without confirmation. If you don't know the current number, find it in `CURRENT_STATUS.md` or ask before writing.
- **Specific claims beat general ones.** "42 students applied in our first full grant cycle" is stronger than "hundreds of students have applied." Specificity builds trust. Vagueness erodes it.
- **No superlatives without qualification.** Unverifiable claims like "the most impactful" or "one of the leading nonprofits" invite skepticism. "Our 2026 cohort of 42 students will receive grants averaging $2,500" is a fact that earns its place.
- **Never invent a data point.** If you need a number and don't have it, use a placeholder and flag it for Eric. A wrong fact published under the Foundation's name does lasting damage to credibility.
