# Language Style Guide — campusready.co

**Purpose:** The definitive reference for how Campus Ready speaks. Every word a user sees — headlines, body copy, buttons, error messages, tooltips, and microcopy — should pass through this guide.  
**Owner:** Copy Specialist  
**Audience:** Any agent (Build, Design, Experience, Copy) writing or reviewing user-facing text  
**Status:** Active — authoritative source for all copy decisions  
**Created:** February 15, 2026

---

## How to Use This Guide

This document is organized for fast lookup. If you're writing a button label, go to **Button Copy**. If you're writing an error message, go to **Error Messages**. If you're unsure whether a line sounds like Campus Ready, run it against the **Voice Qualities** table and the **Word Lists**.

When in doubt, read the line out loud and ask: *Would this feel natural coming from a thoughtful friend who happens to know a lot about college prep?* If yes, it probably works. If it sounds like a marketing email or a chatbot, rewrite it.

## 1. Positioning Statement

*Campus Ready is an intelligent, warmhearted guide who understands how significant — and how stressful — preparing for college can be. We've done the research, compared the options, and organized everything so you don't have to. Our voice is confident but never heavy. There's a lightness to how we communicate — a sense that this process can actually be enjoyable, not just survivable. We're not a peer, not a salesperson, and not an algorithm. We're the trusted, steady presence that helps students and parents navigate this moment with confidence, clarity, and maybe even a little fun along the way.*

**The one-line version:** Friendly experts — warm, confident, effortlessly helpful.

---

## 2. Voice Qualities

Every line of copy should reflect at least two of these qualities. No line should violate any of them.

| Quality | What it means | Sounds like | Doesn't sound like |
|---------|---------------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Intelligent** | We know what we're talking about. Specificity over showing off. | "Based on your style and budget, here's what we found." | "Our proprietary algorithm has analyzed..." |
| **Warm** | We care about the person behind the screen. | "This is a big moment. You must be so proud." | "Please complete the following steps." |
| **Calm** | We relieve stress, never create it. Zero urgency, zero pressure. | "Take your time. Your list will be here." | "Don't miss out! Limited availability!" |
| **Trustworthy** | Plain-spoken. Transparent. No overselling. | "Here's the closest match we found." | "You'll LOVE this amazing deal!" |
| **Empathetic** | We honor the significance of this transition. | "Starting college is a lot. Moving in shouldn't be." | "Get all your dorm essentials now!" |
| **Light** | There's fun in this. The copy smiles without trying hard. | "Now the fun part." / "Nailed it." | "Congratulations on completing your selections!!!" |
| **Ageless** | Equally natural for an 18-year-old and a 50-year-old. | "What's your vibe?" | "What aesthetic resonates with your sensibility?" / "OMG so cute!" |

### The Tiebreaker Rule

**Ageless wins.** When choosing between two versions of any line, pick the one that works for both a smart 18-year-old and a discerning 50-year-old parent. If a line would make the student feel talked down to, cut it. If a line would make the parent feel like they wandered into a teen app, cut it.

---

## 3. What Lightness Looks Like

Lightness is Campus Ready's signature quality. It's the difference between a product that feels like a chore and one that feels like it's on your side. But lightness is about *confidence and ease* — not jokes, not enthusiasm, not performative energy.

**Good lightness:**
- "Now the fun part."
- "Nailed it."
- "Less ironing, more living."
- "Nicely done."
- "What's your vibe?"

**Not lightness — trying too hard:**
- "OMG you're gonna love this!"
- "Let's goooo! 🎉"
- "You're basically a shopping genius!"
- Multiple exclamation points anywhere

**The rule:** Write copy that smiles. Don't write copy that grins.

**How to test it:** If a line would sound odd read aloud in a calm, confident voice, it's probably too much. Campus Ready copy should sound good at low volume.

## 4. Register Guidance

### Student vs. Parent

The warmth stays constant. The framing shifts.

**Students** are spoken to directly. They are the subject.  
**Parents** are spoken to as the shopper. The student is the beneficiary.

The parent path talks *about* the student, not *to the student through the parent*. This is a critical distinction.

| Context | Student | Parent |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Budget question | "What's your budget for dorm shopping?" | "What's the budget for [Name]'s dorm shopping?" |
| Style question | "What's your vibe?" | "What's [Name]'s vibe?" |
| Helper text | "These help us find products you'll love." | "These help us find products [Name] will love." |
| Summary | "You're all set for Penn State." | "[Name]'s all set for Penn State." |
| List references | "Your list" | "[Name]'s list" |

**Parent-specific warmth:** Parents get one emotional acknowledgment early in the flow ("This is a big moment. You must be so proud."). After that, the tone is practical and supportive — we don't re-sentimentalize. One genuine moment lands harder than repeated ones.

### Budget Tiers

The voice doesn't change by tier. The *vocabulary* can shift slightly.

**Economy tier:** Can be more direct. These shoppers appreciate straightforwardness and value. "Quality basics" is the right register — honest, not apologetic.

**Premium tier:** A touch more polish. Families shopping at this level expect sophistication — they know what quality costs. No need to convince them; just present well. "The best we've found" is confident without being fawning.

**Mid tier:** The sweet spot label says it all. Balanced, assured, no explanation needed.

## 5. Sentence Architecture

### Preferred Patterns

**Short + medium pairing.** The strongest copy in the app pairs a short declarative sentence with a medium supporting sentence. This creates rhythm and breathing room.

- "Starting college is a lot. Moving in shouldn't be."
- "Nicely done. Budget, style, materials — locked."
- "Now the fun part. Based on your style and budget, here's what we found."

**Active voice, almost always.** "We found products you'll love" over "Products have been found for you."

**Contractions, always.** "We've" not "We have." "You'll" not "You will." "Here's" not "Here is." Contractions make copy sound like speech. Uncontracted copy sounds like a terms of service.

**Period-terminated confidence.** Periods convey calm authority. The most powerful lines in the app — "Nailed it." "Nicely done." "Now the fun part." — all end with periods, not exclamation points.

### What to Avoid

**Run-on helper text.** If a supporting sentence needs a second clause, it probably needs to be two sentences or to be cut.

**Stacked questions.** Never ask the user more than one question per screen. (See DEC-031: progressive disclosure.)

**Explaining ourselves.** "We use this to personalize your recommendations based on your input" — the user doesn't need to know the mechanism. "This helps us find products you'll love" is sufficient.

## 6. Word Lists

### Words and Phrases We Use

| Word/Phrase | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| "your list" | Natural, personal, lightweight |
| "what we found" | Positions us as having done the work |
| "based on your style" | Reinforces personalization without jargon |
| "here's what we'd recommend" | Confident, specific |
| "you're all set" | Calm completion, not breathless celebration |
| "take your time" | Relieves pressure |
| "no problem" | Easy, non-judgmental (for skip confirmations) |
| "pick" / "choose" | Natural, active |
| "save" | Clear action, no confusion |
| "skip" | Neutral, no guilt |
| "come back anytime" | Reassuring permanence |
| "school" | Not "university" or "institution" or "college" (unless matching an official name) |
| "dorm" | Not "residence hall" (too formal) or "room" (too vague) |

### Words and Phrases We Never Use

| Word/Phrase | Why not | Use instead |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| "shop" (as a noun) | Generic e-commerce | "list" or "picks" |
| "browse our selection" | Retail catalog language | "Here's what we found" |
| "one-stop shop" | Cliché, generic | — (don't replace; cut the concept) |
| "curated" | Overused, pretentious | "picked" or "selected" |
| "hand-picked" | Dishonest at scale | "recommended" |
| "essentials" (as a brand word) | Every competitor uses it | "what you need" or specific category names |
| "journey" | Corporate buzzword | "process" or nothing |
| "don't miss out" | Creates anxiety, salesy | — |
| "limited time" | False urgency | — |
| "amazing" / "incredible" | Overselling | Use specific praise or none |
| "definitely" / "you'll need" | Condescending, presumptuous | "we'd recommend" |
| "oops" / "uh oh" | Cutesy error language | Direct, helpful language |
| "please" (in CTAs) | Unnecessary deference in buttons | Verb-forward: "Save" not "Please save" |
| "submit" | Cold, transactional | "Save" / "Continue" / "Create" |
| "invalid" | Technical jargon | Describe the actual issue |
| "click here" | Inaccessible, dated | Descriptive link text |

## 7. Punctuation & Formatting

### Exclamation Points

**Near-zero policy.** Periods convey more confidence than exclamation points. An exclamation point says "this is exciting!" A period says "this is exciting" — and trusts the reader to feel it.

**Allowed:** One exclamation point per screen, maximum, and only at genuinely celebratory moments. The school confirmation screen ("We've got your [school] packing list!") earns one. Most screens earn zero.

**Never:** Multiple exclamation points in sequence. Exclamation points in error messages. Exclamation points in helper text. Exclamation points on buttons.

### Em Dashes

Use em dashes (—) to insert a clarifying aside or to create a pivot in a sentence. No spaces on either side.

- "Based on your style and budget, here's what we found. Add what you want, skip what you don't — swipe for alternatives."
- "Natural where it counts, practical where it helps — the best of both."

Use sparingly. More than one em dash per paragraph creates choppiness.

### Ellipses

**Loading states only.** "Loading your list..." is the one acceptable use. Never use ellipses in body copy, headlines, or buttons. They introduce uncertainty — the opposite of confidence.

### Commas

Use the Oxford comma. "Budget, style, and materials." Clarity over brevity.

### Ampersands

Acceptable in short labels and UI elements: "Fabrics & Formulas," "Classic & Clean," "Save & Return." In body copy, spell out "and."

## 8. Emoji Policy

### Where Emojis Belong

**Option cards and preference pills.** Emojis serve as visual anchors in selection UI — they help users scan and differentiate options quickly. 🎓 on the student card, 🌿 on the natural materials option, ☁️ on the soft pillow. These are functional, not decorative.

### Where Emojis Don't Belong

**Headlines, body copy, CTAs, helper text, error messages, or any primary copy.** The voice doesn't need emojis to carry warmth. The words do that work.

**School celebration text** is the one exception — a school-specific emoji in the confirmation moment ("Hook 'em! 🤘") adds personality that would be hard to achieve otherwise. This is earned by the emotional context of the moment.

### The Test

If removing the emoji would make the meaning less clear or the option harder to scan, keep it. If removing it would change nothing, cut it.

## 9. Capitalization

### Sentence Case — Everywhere

**Recommendation: Sentence case for all UI text.** Headlines, subheads, body copy, buttons, links, labels, helper text — all sentence case.

**Rationale:** Sentence case is more conversational, easier to read, and consistent with the warm, approachable voice. Title Case Creates A Subtle Formality That Works Against Our Tone. The best lines in the app already use sentence case naturally: "What's your vibe?" "Now the fun part." "Less ironing, more living."

**Exceptions:**
- Proper nouns (school names, "Campus Ready")
- The word after a colon when it begins a complete sentence
- Badge labels that function as category tags ("Required," "Suggested") — these are capitalized as labels, not as sentences

### Brand Name

"Campus Ready" — two words, both capitalized. Never "CampusReady," "campus ready," or "CR" in user-facing copy. "CR" is acceptable in internal documentation only.

## 10. Button Copy

### Pattern: Verb-Forward, 2–4 Words

Buttons should start with an action verb and be short enough to scan instantly. The verb tells the user what will happen; the object gives context.

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| **Action + object** | "See your list" / "Save for later" / "Create account" |
| **Let's + verb** | "Let's do this" / "Let's go" |
| **State confirmation** | "Continue" / "Save & return" |
| **Destination** | "Continue to Bath" / "Continue to shopping" |

### Rules

- **No exclamation points** on buttons. Ever.
- **No "please"** — buttons are invitations, not requests.
- **No "submit"** — too transactional. Use specific verbs: "Create," "Save," "Send."
- **Consistent within a flow.** If the primary CTA pattern is "Continue," maintain it across screens. Don't alternate between "Continue," "Next," and "Let's go" for the same action type.
- **The arrow icon (â†’) implies forward progression.** Buttons with arrows should communicate movement: "Continue," "See your list," "Let's go." Don't pair an arrow with a non-directional action like "Save."

### CTA Hierarchy

**Primary CTAs** are the main action per screen — bold, prominent, verb-forward.  
**Secondary CTAs** are alternatives — softer in visual weight, still clear. "Save for later" / "I'll do this later."  
**Tertiary actions** are links, not buttons — "Skip this question" / "Edit selections."

## 11. Error Messages

### Pattern: Acknowledge + Solution

Never blame the user. Never use technical jargon. Tell them what happened and what to do next.

**Structure:** [What went wrong, in plain language]. [What to do about it.]

| Situation | Message |
|-----------|---------|
| Sign-in failed | "That didn't work. Check your email and password and try again." |
| Email already in use | "This email already has an account. Try signing in instead." |
| Weak password | "Password needs to be at least 6 characters." |
| Generic failure | "Something went wrong on our end. Try again in a moment." |
| Network error | "Looks like you're offline. Reconnect and try again." |
| School not found | "We don't have that school yet, but you can use a general list instead." |

### Rules

- **No "oops," "uh oh," "oh no," or "whoops."** These infantilize the user and make errors feel whimsical rather than addressed.
- **No "invalid," "error," "failed," or technical codes.** Translate every error into human language.
- **No exclamation points.** Errors should feel calm and competent.
- **Always offer a path forward.** Even if the path is "try again."
- **Sentence case, period-terminated.** Consistent with the rest of the voice.

## 12. Empty States

### Pattern: Encouraging, Not Apologetic

When there's nothing to show, the copy should guide — never shrug.

| State | Message |
|-------|---------|
| No search results | "We don't have that school yet. Try searching by full name, or use a general list." |
| Empty cart | "Nothing here yet. Head back to shopping to add items." |
| No items in category | "No items in this category match your preferences. We'll keep looking." |
| List not saved | "Your list isn't saved yet. Create an account to keep it." |

### Rules

- **Never say "no results found."** This is a system message, not a human one.
- **Never apologize.** "Sorry, we couldn't find anything" puts Campus Ready in a diminished position. State the situation, offer the next step.
- **Keep it short.** Empty states should be concise — one or two sentences maximum.

## 13. Number & Currency Formatting

### Currency

| Context | Format | Example | Rationale |
|---------|--------|---------|-----------|
| Summary stat line | Rounded to whole dollar | "$488" | Clean, scannable at a glance |
| Floating cart total | Rounded to whole dollar | "$488" | Matches summary for consistency |
| Line-item detail | Two decimal places | "$487.92" | Precision matters when evaluating individual items |
| Price ranges (budget tiers) | Rounded | "Under $400" / "$400–700" | Clean, easy to compare |

### Numbers

- Spell out one through nine in body copy. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use numerals for item counts in UI: "12 items," not "twelve items."
- Use numerals with units: "3 quick questions," "6+ characters."
- Use an en dash (–), not a hyphen (-), for ranges: "$400–700."

### Item Counts

Format: "[number] items" (lowercase, no period). When paired with currency: "12 items · $488" — a middle dot (·) as separator, spaces on both sides.

## 14. Terminology Glossary

These terms have specific meanings in the Campus Ready context. Use them consistently.

| Term | Use for | Don't use |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **list** | The user's personalized collection of items to shop for. Primary user-facing term. | "cart" (until checkout), "basket" |
| **checklist** | The school-provided set of recommended items. Used in the selection phase where users check off what they need. | Don't use interchangeably with "list" |
| **packing list** | The school's official housing document. Used only when referencing the school's source material: "View official packing list." | Don't use as a general synonym for "list" |
| **shopping list** | The finalized list on the summary screen, ready for purchase. Used in the summary context: "Your shopping list." | Don't use during the checklist selection phase |
| **picks** / **recommendations** | What Campus Ready suggests. "Here's what we found" / "our picks." | "curated selections," "hand-picked items" |
| **preferences** | The user's budget, style, and material choices. | "settings," "options," "profile" |
| **school** | The institution. Default term in user-facing copy. | "university," "college," "institution" (unless matching an official name) |
| **dorm** | The living space. | "residence hall" (too formal), "room" (too vague) |
| **items** | Individual products. | "products" (in user-facing copy — too commercial), "goods" |
| **add** / **added** | The action of selecting an item for the shopping list. | "add to cart" (no cart concept during shopping flow) |
| **skip** | Declining a question or category. Neutral verb, no guilt. | "dismiss," "ignore," "pass" |

### Consistency Note

The transition from "checklist" to "list" to "shopping list" mirrors the user's journey: they start by reviewing what the school recommends (checklist), select what they need (list), and finalize what they're buying (shopping list). This progression should feel natural, not jarring — the terminology evolves with the context.

## 16. Skip & Confirmation Copy

### Skip Links

Skip links should be gentle invitations, never guilt-inducing. The user always has permission to move on.

**Pattern:** "Skip this question" — direct, neutral, no consequences implied.

### Skip Confirmation Modal

When a user chooses to skip, confirm with warmth and reassurance.

**Headline:** "Skip this one?"  
**Body:** "No problem — we'll pick something great. You can change anytime."

### Rules

- Never say "Are you sure?" — this questions the user's judgment.
- Never list what they'll miss — this creates anxiety.
- Always mention that the choice is reversible when it is.
- Keep confirmation modals to headline + one sentence + two buttons.

## 17. Trust Sentences

Trust sentences appear on product cards in the shopping flow. Each product gets one sentence that builds confidence in the recommendation. These are not feature lists — they're editorial judgments.

### Pattern

One sentence. Specific. Connects the product to the user's context or to a concrete quality signal.

**Good trust sentences:**
- "Top-rated by students for softness and durability."
- "The best-reviewed option in your price range."
- "Premium cotton — chosen for your natural materials preference."
- "A consistent favorite across 400+ college checklists."

**Not trust sentences:**
- "Great product!" (empty praise)
- "This is a 100% cotton sheet set with 400 thread count." (feature list, not trust)
- "Customers love this!" (generic, unsupported)
- "You'll love this." (presumptuous)

### Rules

- One sentence. No compound sentences with semicolons.
- Must reference either the user's preferences, a verifiable quality signal, or community validation.
- Never make price promises or guarantees.
- Never use superlatives without qualification ("best" needs a frame: "best-reviewed in your price range" — not "the best sheets").

## 18. Accessibility Copy

### Alt Text

Every image needs meaningful alt text. Product images: "[Product name] — [key visual detail]." Decorative images: empty alt attribute (alt="").

### Aria Labels

Interactive elements that aren't self-describing need aria-labels. A back arrow button: aria-label="Go back." A close button (×): aria-label="Close."

### Screen Reader Text

Progress indicators: "Category 3 of 10." Item counts: "12 items selected." Cart totals: "$488 total."

### Focus Management

When a screen transitions, focus should move to the new screen's primary heading or first interactive element. This is a Build concern, but Copy should ensure that headings and labels make sense when read in isolation by a screen reader.

## 19. Legal Sensitivity Flags

The following patterns should be flagged for legal review before launch. This guide does not provide legal advice — it identifies copy that could create liability.

**Flag for review:**
- Any copy that implies a price guarantee or price matching
- Any copy that says "official" (as in "official checklist") — confirm we have rights to reference school materials
- Any copy that promises savings ("save up to X%")
- Any copy about data handling ("we don't share your data" — verify with privacy policy)
- "A Campus Ready initiative" in the footer — confirm the legal relationship between campusready.co and campusready.foundation is accurately represented
- Trust sentences that make quality claims ("best," "top-rated") — ensure they're substantiated

## Quick Reference Card

For fast decisions, use this simplified checklist:

**Does it sound like Campus Ready?**
- [ ] Could a confident, warm friend say this?
- [ ] Would it work for both a student and a parent?
- [ ] Does it end with a period, not an exclamation point?
- [ ] Is it under 20 words? (If not, can it be?)
- [ ] Does it use a contraction where one is possible?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon, cliché, and e-commerce language?

**If you're unsure, model it after these:**
- "Starting college is a lot. Moving in shouldn't be."
- "What's your vibe?"
- "Now the fun part."
- "Nailed it."
- "Less ironing, more living."
- "Already have something? Leave it unchecked."
