Who We Are

Campus Ready Foundation is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit founded in 2025 by Eric Lilavois and Karen Dantzler. The Foundation provides direct-fulfillment grants to first-time college students from low-income households: move-in essentials, travel to campus arranged and paid, meal support for the early days before dining services open, and a gift card for incidentals. The grant is not cash. Every student receives exactly what they need to arrive prepared, with confidence, and dignity, ready to start strong.

Financial aid was designed to cover college tuition, room, and board. It was not designed to cover living away from home, or the flight to a campus 1,500 miles away, or the costs that mount the week before classes start. For many students, those costs arrive without warning and without a solution. That is the moment Campus Ready was built for.

The 2026 cohort is our first full class: 41 students heading to 34 colleges across the country. We received 45 completed applications; three came in after the deadline and one was ineligible under program criteria. Every eligible, on-time applicant was awarded.

The Program

In addition to grants, award recipients are invited to an Orientation & Celebration event mid-summer during which they will learn what to expect as they prepare for their move. Students receive their travel and meal credits, learn from speakers on how to travel, what to ship ahead, and what to expect on move-in day.

Move-in essentials Products like bedding, bath, and desk supplies, and personal care necessities are fulfilled before arrival.
Travel to campus Door-to-door: Lyft to the airport, flight, and Lyft to campus. For local students, a direct Lyft ride. For one student this year, a road-trip gas card. When a parent or guardian travels with the student, we cover their airfare and hotel, too.
Early-arrival support A DoorDash Community Credit redeemable across the full DoorDash platform — meals, groceries, and essentials during the days before campus dining opens.
Orientation & Celebration An Orientation & Celebration event where students receive their travel and meal credits, learn from speakers on how to travel, what to expect at move-in, and connect with each other and the Foundation team before heading to campus.

The Need

Nearly half of the students in this cohort live below the federal poverty line on a per-person basis. More than two-thirds reported at least one significant hardship circumstance — housing instability, a parent out of work, no internet at home, earnings going back into the household instead of toward college. Most reported more than one.

And then there are the students the federal aid system misses entirely. A Student Aid Index is a formula. It measures reported income against household size. It doesn’t measure what a family can actually spare, or what a student quietly carries. One applicant had an SAI of 7,359, above the federal maximum-need threshold. She also works, and every dollar she earns goes back into her household for groceries, medical bills, and rent. Her parents are immigrants; her father left school at age six. There is no discretionary money for her move to a California university. Her SAI says otherwise. Her life does not.

These are the students Campus Ready was built for.

The Students

Three profiles from the 2026 application pool. Each represents a distinct dimension of need. Together, they make the case for why this program exists.

Jocelyn R. · Vallejo, CA → Chico State

Jocelyn grew up in a household of six in Vallejo on $20,000 a year. Her family lost their home in 2016. After that, her older brother stepped in as the household’s financial anchor — until he died of leukemia in late 2023. She carries a Student Aid Index of −1500, the federal government’s designation for maximum financial need, and she’s heading to Chico State to study social work and public health.

“I plan to study Social Work and Public Health… I am motivated to help people because I know what it is like to be in that position of losing your home, and want to use my experiences to help others. Families deserve a place they can call home without worries.”
— Jocelyn R., Vallejo, CA

The experience of losing a home, and then a brother, didn’t turn inward. It became a direction.

Antonio R. · Santa Rosa, CA → UC Santa Cruz

Antonio has worked 20 to 25 hours a week since he was 16 — not for spending money, as he writes directly in his application, but for survival. In early 2026, his household of two in Santa Rosa moved three times in a matter of months, forced out by financial instability. He sold his car for immediate cash. His reported household income is $18,839 and his SAI is −1500.

“Ever since I turned 16, working hasn’t been a choice for me, it’s been about survival… my living situation has been entirely disrupted. Due to severe financial instability and my mom’s weak job security, we have had to pack up and move homes three separate times in just a matter of months.”
— Antonio R., Santa Rosa, CA

He’s heading to UC Santa Cruz this fall. The instability that shaped him isn’t fully behind him. Arriving prepared won’t change that. It will change how he begins.

Licendi C. · Clearlake, CA → UC Merced

Licendi’s household of four in Clearlake reports $40,000 in annual income — enough to push her Student Aid Index to 7,359, above the federal maximum-need threshold. But an SAI doesn’t describe what a family can actually spare. Both parents are immigrants; her father left school at age six. Licendi works, and everything she earns goes back into the household: groceries, bills, medical expenses. There’s no discretionary money for a move to UC Merced.

“My parents are migrants, and neither of them had the opportunity to continue their education… Although I currently have a job, all of my earnings go toward supporting my family with basic necessities such as groceries, household bills, and medical expenses. My parents crossed a border to give me a chance at the future they could only dream of.”
— Licendi C., Clearlake, CA

She plans to study neuroscience and pursue medicine. Her SAI is the highest of the three profiles here yet her need is no less real. The nuance of her financial profile is the type the federal system misses.

Partners

Campus Ready’s corporate partners provide what each student needs in various forms: cash support helps fund the program, and in-kind contributions deliver products and services directly to students. Both matter. Both are welcome.

Target provided the Foundation’s first corporate grant, supporting the 2026 cohort at a critical stage of our launch year. Lyft provided ride credits covering every student’s door-to-door travel on move-in day. DoorDash provided Community Credits redeemable across their full platform, ensuring every student has meals and groceries covered during the early days before campus dining opens.

In addition to donations we are actively seeking in-kind corporate partners in three categories: airlines, retail & home goods, and hospitality. If your organization supports education, community and/or social equality in any way, let’s find a way to work together.

Contact Eric Lilavois at eric@campusready.org.

2026 Financials

The 2026 program will serve our 41 award recipients completely. Operating expenses represent less than 3% of total program cost. Partner credits from Lyft and DoorDash reduce the cash cost of each grant directly and are reflected at applied values. A travel contingency provides a 15% buffer for itinerary changes and last-minute adjustments on move-in day.
